Cleaning Historic Homes in New England: A Complete Guide

January 10, 2025 7 min read

Massachusetts is home to some of the oldest and most beautiful historic homes in America. From stately Victorians in Worcester to charming Cape Cods on the coast, these homes require special cleaning techniques to preserve their character while keeping them healthy and beautiful.

Understanding Historic Home Architecture

Different architectural styles common in Massachusetts present unique cleaning challenges:

Colonial Homes (1600s-1700s)

Found throughout Massachusetts, especially in historic towns like Concord and Lexington:

  • Wide plank floors: Original pine or oak floors need gentle cleaning without excessive moisture
  • Plaster walls: Avoid heavy scrubbing that can damage historic lime plaster
  • Small-paned windows: Each pane requires individual attention
  • Large fireplaces: Significant soot and ash management
  • Low ceilings with exposed beams: Careful dusting to avoid damage

Victorian Homes (1837-1901)

Common in cities like Worcester, Newton, and Brookline:

  • Ornate woodwork: Detailed moldings, wainscoting, and built-ins require careful dusting
  • Stained glass windows: Never use ammonia-based cleaners
  • High ceilings: Require extension tools and sometimes ladders
  • Elaborate light fixtures: Crystal chandeliers need delicate cleaning
  • Pocket doors: Tracks accumulate dust and need regular attention

Cape Cod Style (1930s-present)

The quintessential New England home:

  • Steep rooflines: Attic spaces prone to dust and temperature extremes
  • Dormers: Small windows that require careful cleaning
  • Cedar shingle exteriors: Special care to avoid damage during window cleaning
  • Compact layouts: Tight spaces require efficient cleaning techniques

Cleaning Historic Woodwork

Wood is the defining feature of most historic New England homes. Here's how to care for it properly:

Daily Maintenance

  • Use a soft, dry microfiber cloth for dusting
  • Work with the grain, never against it
  • Avoid silicone-based dusting sprays that can build up over time

Deep Cleaning

  • Mix a few drops of Murphy's Oil Soap in warm water
  • Wring cloth until nearly dry before wiping wood
  • Dry immediately with a clean cloth
  • Apply quality furniture wax once or twice yearly

What to Avoid

  • Never use all-purpose cleaners on historic wood
  • Avoid excessive water - it can damage finishes and cause warping
  • Don't use abrasive sponges or steel wool

Caring for Historic Floors

Wide Plank Hardwood

Original wide plank floors are treasures worth preserving:

  • Sweep or vacuum regularly with a hard floor attachment
  • Damp mop occasionally - never wet mop
  • Place felt pads under all furniture
  • Use natural floor wax, not polyurethane-based products

Original Tile Work

Victorian and early 20th century homes often feature decorative tile:

  • Use pH-neutral cleaners only
  • Avoid acidic cleaners that can etch historic tile
  • Re-seal grout annually to prevent staining

Window Care in Historic Homes

Old windows require special attention:

  • Multi-pane windows: Clean each pane individually, taking care around old glazing
  • Stained glass: Use distilled water and mild dish soap only - never ammonia
  • Wavy antique glass: Clean gently to avoid cracking
  • Sash windows: Clean tracks and lubricate hardware annually

Managing Dust in Older Homes

Historic homes often have more dust due to their age and construction:

  • Gaps in old construction allow more outside air (and dust) inside
  • Plaster walls can generate fine dust as they age
  • Old insulation materials may contribute to airborne particles

Solutions

  • Use HEPA filtration vacuums
  • Change HVAC filters frequently
  • Consider portable air purifiers for bedrooms
  • Regular professional deep cleaning to stay ahead of accumulation

Preservation vs. Deep Cleaning

Finding the balance between thorough cleaning and preservation:

  • Less is more: Use the gentlest effective method
  • Test first: Always test cleaning products in an inconspicuous area
  • Consult experts: For significant cleaning of historic features, consider consulting a preservation specialist
  • Document: Keep records of cleaning products and methods that work well

Your historic home is a piece of Massachusetts history. With proper care and cleaning, it will continue to be beautiful for generations to come.
