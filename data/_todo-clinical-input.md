# Clinical input needed from Jeneva Thomas

**Why this document exists.** The service pages need two things that cannot be
looked up: what surveyors actually write up, and what a cleaning protocol really
looks like in each facility type. Regulations are public and already filled in
below. Twenty-two years inside Massachusetts clinical environments is not, and
it is the only part of this site a competitor cannot copy.

**Nothing here will be invented.** Any question left blank means that block does
not render. A page with four honest sections beats a page with eight where half
are guesses — and on a site selling regulatory compliance, an invented survey
finding is the one mistake that would cost a contract.

**How to answer.** Speak plainly, in your own words. Bullet fragments are fine.
Portuguese is fine — it will be translated for the site. Do not worry about
polish; specificity is what matters. "The surveyor pulled back the privacy
curtain and found dust on the top rail" is worth more than three paragraphs of
general advice.

## For every service — the four core questions

Answer these once per facility type below.

**Q1. What do surveyors actually cite?**
Think of the real write-ups you have seen — Joint Commission, DPH, CMS, AAAHC,
or an internal audit. What specifically got flagged? Where were they looking
that people do not expect? Six examples per facility type is the target.

**Q2. What is the cleaning sequence, room by room?**
Walk through it as you would train a new hire on day one. Which surface first
and why, what changes between rooms, when cloths and mop heads get swapped,
what gets done daily versus weekly versus terminal.

**Q3. What do people get wrong?**
The mistake you see in facility after facility — the shortcut that looks fine
and is not, the product used at the wrong dwell time, the area everyone skips.

**Q4. What does the administrator receive from you, on paper?**
Logs, checklists, sign-offs, ATP results, incident reports. What is handed over,
how often, and what do they do with it during a survey?

## 1. Medical Office Cleaning

*Buyer: Practice Manager*

**Regulation (already sourced — confirm nothing is missing):**
- OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard, 29 CFR 1910.1030
- CDC Guidelines for Environmental Infection Control in Health-Care Facilities
- EPA List N registered disinfectants
- 105 CMR 140 — Massachusetts DPH, Licensure of Clinics

**Beyond Q1–Q4:**
- Exam room turnover between patients — what is the actual sequence?
- Blood pressure cuffs, otoscopes, exam table paper — who cleans what, you or clinical staff? Where is that line drawn in the contract?
- Sharps containers and regulated medical waste — what is your role and what is explicitly not?

## 2. Dental Office Cleaning

*Buyer: Office Manager / practice-owner dentist*

**Regulation:**
- CDC Guidelines for Infection Control in Dental Health-Care Settings (2003) and the 2016 Summary
- 234 CMR — Massachusetts Board of Registration in Dentistry
- OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1030

**Beyond Q1–Q4:**
- Operatory turnover: what is EVS versus what is dental assistant?
- Dental unit waterlines — do you touch them at all? If not, say so plainly; it belongs in the contract scope.
- Aerosol settling time after a procedure — how long before you enter, and where did that number come from?
- Sterilization area: clean side versus dirty side — how do you avoid crossing it?

## 4. Rehabilitation Clinic Cleaning

*Buyer: Clinic Director (PT / OT / speech)*

**Regulation:**
- CDC ambulatory care infection prevention guidance
- 105 CMR 140

This is the facility type most people underestimate. Direct skin contact with
shared equipment, all day, with no barrier.

**Beyond Q1–Q4:**
- Treatment tables, mats, therabands, weights, exercise balls, parallel bars, treadmills — what is the frequency and product for each?
- Between-patient wipe-down: what is the therapist's job and what is yours?
- Hydrotherapy or aquatic therapy — have you handled it? Different rules entirely.
- Have you seen MRSA or fungal issues traced to shared equipment? What happened?

## 5. Skilled Nursing & Long-Term Care Cleaning

*Buyer: Director of Nursing / Administrator*

**Regulation:**
- 105 CMR 150 — Massachusetts DPH, Standards for Long Term Care Facilities
- CMS Requirements of Participation, F-tags
- CDC guidance for C. difficile and norovirus in long-term care

Highest stakes, longest sales cycle, biggest contract.

**Beyond Q1–Q4:**
- Outbreak response — walk through a real one. C. diff, norovirus, influenza. What changed hour by hour?
- Which F-tags have you seen cited for environmental issues?
- Bleach versus quat for C. diff: what do you use, at what dilution, at what dwell time?
- Resident rooms versus common areas versus dining versus assisted bathing — different protocols?
- How do you clean around a resident who cannot leave the room?

## 8. Urgent Care Center Cleaning

*Buyer: Site Manager*

**Regulation:**
- OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1030
- CDC Guidelines for Environmental Infection Control

The defining constraint is speed: rooms turn in minutes, and disinfectant dwell
time does not shrink to fit.

**Beyond Q1–Q4:**
- How do you hold the dwell time when the room is needed in five minutes?
- Which product do you use here specifically, and what is its contact time?
- Triage and waiting areas during flu season — what changes?
- Have you handled a room after a suspected infectious patient? What was different?
